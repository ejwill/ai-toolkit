# Tool Icon Design Brief

Use this brief when creating new Milwaukee-style tool icons for latches and labels.

## Purpose

These icons are not product renderings. They are small-format product markers that need to read instantly on a printed part.

## Visual Direction

- Bold and simplified
- Clearly product-inspired
- High contrast
- Suitable for a black latch body with white outline and red accents
- Consistent across different tools

## Core Style Rules

- Start from a recognizable silhouette.
- Keep the icon upright or horizontal in the source module.
- Use a continuous white outline around the overall tool shape.
- Treat the black regions as exposed latch material, not as a separate printed icon part.
- Use a few red accent regions to suggest Milwaukee styling.
- Prefer 1 strong silhouette over many small details.

## What To Keep

- The most recognizable outer profile features
- Major grip or battery shape
- One or two major functional landmarks such as a rod, barrel, chuck, or blade
- A small number of interior accents that support recognition

## What To Remove

- Logos and text
- Fine vent lines
- Small screws and fasteners
- Tiny teeth unless they are essential to the category
- Photo-real bevels and tiny contour changes
- Detail that becomes ambiguous at latch scale

## Color Model

- Black: exposed latch body, not a generated insert
- White: outer stroke or white inset regions
- Red: housing panels, grip stripes, battery accents, or signature brand-style regions

The red should feel intentional and sparse. It should not try to fill every visible body panel.
The white should usually define the icon's outer read.

## Shape Constraints

- Avoid tiny floating islands
- Avoid very thin slivers
- Avoid narrow negative spaces that will close up during printing
- Prefer large geometric relationships over subtle contour fidelity

## Consistency Targets

Across the icon set, aim for:

- similar outline thickness
- similar simplification level
- similar red-to-white balance
- similar handling of negative space
- comparable visual weight when scaled to the same height

## Acceptance Test

An icon is ready when:

- it is recognizable from the white layer alone or from white+red together
- it still reads when reduced to actual use size
- it feels like part of the same family as the other tool icons
- it looks intentional rather than traced

If it looks accurate but not icon-like, simplify it again.
