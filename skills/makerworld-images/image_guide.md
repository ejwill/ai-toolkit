# MakerWorld Image Guide

This guide captures the repository conventions for making MakerWorld listing images, covers, gallery shots, comparison images, and GIFs.

## 1. What the images should do

Images should answer the three questions a buyer has immediately:

1. What is it?
2. What does it look like on the real product?
3. How hard is it to print or use?

### Core goals

- Make the model instantly recognizable.
- Show scale and fit.
- Show the best visual detail close up.
- Reduce uncertainty about orientation or assembly.
- Make the gallery feel useful, not decorative.

## 2. Recommended image types

### Hero image

- Use as the first image on the listing page.
- Keep the subject large enough to read on mobile.
- Favor a clean, centered composition.
- Show the model in its best, clearest orientation.

### App cover

- Treat this as a vertical crop problem first and a photo problem second.
- Keep important geometry away from the top and bottom edges.
- Leave extra space around the subject so the crop can breathe.
- Verify the subject still reads well when cropped to a tall frame.

### Detail shot

- Show the most important feature up close.
- Use this for icons, texture, fit details, or printed text.
- Crop tightly enough that the viewer can inspect the craftsmanship.

### In-use photo

- Show the model attached to the actual product whenever possible.
- This is the best place to prove fit and scale.
- Include enough context that the viewer can tell what the model is for.

### Comparison shot

- Use side-by-side images when the product has clear variants.
- Good examples are multi-color vs press-fit, raised vs engraved, or before vs after.
- Keep the comparison label simple and legible.

### GIF or short motion clip

- Use motion when the model has a transformation, installation step, or visual change.
- Keep the loop short and easy to understand without sound.
- Favor one clear action over a complicated montage.

## 3. Composition rules

### Framing

- Keep the model large in the frame.
- Avoid tiny objects lost in a busy scene.
- Center the subject unless a deliberate off-center crop improves the story.

### Background

- Use a background that helps the model stand out.
- Avoid clutter that competes with the object.
- Keep the background consistent across a product set when possible.

### Lighting

- Use enough light to show edges and print detail.
- Avoid harsh glare on glossy surfaces.
- Make sure text and badges stay readable.

### Consistency

- Use similar angles for similar products.
- Keep the visual language stable across covers and gallery images.
- If there are multiple products in a family, make the gallery feel related.

## 4. Editing and export

### Basic editing

- Crop for the target format instead of relying on a default shot.
- Adjust exposure so the model is easy to inspect.
- Keep colors believable unless the image is explicitly stylized.

### File hygiene

- Prefer clear file names.
- Keep source photos separate from final export files.
- Export final assets in the formats the listing actually needs.

## 5. Suggested gallery sequence

1. Hero image.
2. Detail shot.
3. In-use shot.
4. Variant or comparison.
5. Installation or assembly step.
6. GIF if available.

## 6. Practical checklist

- The first image reads well on mobile.
- The cover crop still looks good in a tall frame.
- At least one image proves fit on the actual product.
- At least one image shows the detail people care about most.
- The gallery tells a story instead of just repeating angles.
- Any GIF adds information rather than noise.
